About IEEE C57.104-2019

IEEE C57.104-2019 is a standard for the interpretation of dissolved gas analysis (DGA) in transformer oil. It provides guidelines for the detection and interpretation of gases dissolved in transformer oil, which can indicate the presence of potential problems in the transformer such as overheating, arcing, or aging of the insulation. The standard includes information on the normal levels of dissolved gases, as well as the levels that indicate potential problems. It also provides guidelines for the interpretation of DGA results, including the use of ratio analysis to determine the potential cause of any abnormalities. Additionally, it provides guidance on sampling, testing, and reporting of DGA results.

DGA Analysis Tools Used in this Website

There are many popular methods to assess the DGA reports like key gas method, rogers ratio, duval triangles and pentagon method. Among them here in this website we have particularly put our focus on rogers ratios and duval triangles (1,4,5) method, as manual assessment and detection of fault types using these methods are quite tedious and time consuming, specially when you have to analyze many reports. But gradually we will include other analysis in this website as well.

Rogers Ratios

IEEE C57.104-2019 standard includes the use of Rogers Ratios, which are a set of mathematical ratios used to interpret the results of DGA testing. The ratios are used to determine the level of degradation or fault within the transformer, and are calculated by dividing the concentration of a specific dissolved gas by the concentration of another specific gas.

Duval Triangles

Duval's triangle is a graphical representation of the performance characteristics of a generator or motor as defined in the IEEE C57.104-2019 DGA (Dissolved Gas Analysis) standard. The triangle is divided into three regions, each representing a different type of electrical equipment failure.
The triangles (1,4,5) are used to interpret dissolved gas analysis results and to identify potential issues with electrical equipment. The triangles are also used to compare results from different samples and to determine if a problem is getting worse over time.
Note That:

  • a) Triangles 4 and 5 should never be used for faults identified first with Triangle 1 as electrical faults D1 or D2.

  • b) Triangle 4 should be used only in case of faults identified first as faults PD, T1 or T2 in Triangle 1.

  • c) Triangle 5 should be used only in case of faults identified first as faults T2 or T3 in Triangle 1
